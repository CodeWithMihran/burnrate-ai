# ECONOMICS

## Economic framing

The economic case for BurnRate.ai is not that every completed audit becomes a customer. The case is that the product can cheaply surface the subset of teams whose AI-tool spend is disorganized enough to justify a real follow-up.

That means the product should be evaluated as a lead-generation and qualification asset, not just as a free calculator.

## What a converted customer could be worth

Assume Credex eventually helps a converted customer route around $1,500 per month of AI spend through a more favorable buying path, and assume Credex captures a conservative 15% gross margin on that volume.

That would imply:

- monthly gross profit per converted customer: about $225
- annualized gross profit: about $2,700

If the average retained customer lasts 18 months, gross-profit LTV is roughly:

- $4,050

This is not meant to be a precise internal forecast. It is meant to show that even modest conversion can make the product financially plausible.

## Why the lead quality matters more than top-of-funnel volume

A weak version of this product could drive plenty of curiosity clicks without creating real value. That is why the economics should anchor around qualified opportunities rather than raw audit count.

One high-savings startup team that can actually move spend is more valuable than dozens of casual users with tiny or irrelevant savings opportunities.

## Early CAC assumptions

The likely early channels are:

- founder/engineering-manager outbound
- teardown-style content
- community distribution
- product-led sharing of results

That leads to a very different CAC picture than paid acquisition.

### Outbound-assisted CAC

Cash cost is low, but time cost is real. If one operator can send 80 targeted messages and book 4 serious product users, the implied CAC is mostly labor, not paid media. A reasonable early modeled CAC range is:

- $20 to $40 implied CAC

### Community / content CAC

This is still time-heavy, but cheaper in direct cash terms. A reasonable early modeled range is:

- $10 to $25 implied CAC

### Product-led / share-led CAC

If the public report becomes screenshot-worthy and naturally shareable, the marginal acquisition cost can become very low. A reasonable early aspiration is:

- below $10 implied CAC

The important point is not the exact dollar. It is that the product has a plausible path to low-CAC growth because the report itself can become a distribution object.

## Funnel model

A realistic early funnel could look something like this:

- 100 completed audits
- 25 captured reports or follow-up submissions
- 8 qualified high-intent conversations
- 2 paying customers

That implies:

- 25% audit-to-capture
- 8% audit-to-qualified-conversation
- 2% audit-to-paid
- 25% qualified-conversation-to-paid

For an early B2B wedge, that is not unreasonable if the product is actually surfacing meaningful overspend rather than vanity calculations.

## Why that can still be attractive

Using the rough LTV assumption of $4,050 gross-profit value per converted customer, even a $100 CAC would still be attractive.

If the tool can acquire or qualify customers below that threshold, it becomes a very plausible internal acquisition asset for Credex.

## What has to be true for the economics to hold

The economics only work if three conditions stay true:

1. the audit identifies real savings opportunities
2. a meaningful share of those opportunities belong to teams Credex can actually help
3. the lead capture and follow-up flow does not feel too salesy too early

If any of those fail, the numbers get worse quickly. A product that produces attention but not qualified opportunities is not economically interesting.

## A simple 18-month scale thought experiment

If Credex wanted this channel to drive roughly $1M of ARR-equivalent contribution over 18 months, a simple way to think about it is:

- monthly gross profit target: about $83K
- estimated monthly gross profit per active converted customer: about $225

That suggests the business would need roughly:

- 370 active converted customers

If audit-to-paid conversion stayed around 2%, that would imply the need for roughly:

- 18,500 completed audits over the period

or about:

- 1,000 completed audits per month at later-stage steady state

That is ambitious, but not unrealistic if the product earns organic sharing and finds the right founder/operator distribution loops.

## Why this is a good assignment economics story

The strongest economic story for BurnRate.ai is not "this becomes a huge standalone business immediately." It is:

- the product is cheap to run
- the product qualifies users before humans spend time on them
- the product has a credible wedge into real buying behavior
- the product creates a measurable bridge between free utility and monetizable follow-up

That is exactly the kind of economics story that makes the project feel more like a product opportunity and less like a classroom calculator.
