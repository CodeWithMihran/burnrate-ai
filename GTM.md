# GTM

## The wedge

BurnRate.ai should not be positioned as a generic AI productivity tool or a broad startup-finance dashboard. Its strongest wedge is much narrower: a free AI spend audit for startup teams that have adopted multiple AI subscriptions faster than they have built discipline around them.

That positioning matters because the product is not trying to solve all software spend. It is trying to solve a specific modern founder problem:

- too many overlapping AI subscriptions
- unclear seat-to-seat fit
- retail pricing paid informally across multiple tools
- no procurement owner forcing consistency

In that context, BurnRate.ai is not just a calculator. It is a fast second opinion on whether the current stack is bloated, mismatched, or already reasonably efficient.

## Who the product is for

The best initial user is not "startups" in the abstract. It is one of three people inside an early-stage software company:

- a founder who still feels the credit-card pain personally
- an engineering manager trying to standardize tool choices across the team
- a technical operator or chief-of-staff type person who has inherited messy tool spend without a real procurement function

The company profile is usually:

- seed to Series A
- roughly 5 to 40 employees
- using two to five AI tools across coding, writing, research, or API workloads
- paying for those tools through informal team adoption rather than a centralized buying process

This is the sweet spot because the team is large enough to leak money, but still small enough that nobody has time to build an internal spend workflow.

## The moment of intent

The strongest moment to capture a user is not "I want better AI." It is:

- "why are we suddenly paying this much for AI tools?"
- "do we really need both Cursor and Copilot?"
- "is ChatGPT Team actually the right plan for us?"
- "why is every engineer expensing a different assistant?"

That means the product is best introduced when the user is already worried about spend discipline, not when they are browsing abstract productivity tools.

## What they are likely searching for

High-intent search and social-entry queries are likely to look like:

- ChatGPT Team vs Enterprise pricing
- Claude Team pricing
- Cursor vs Copilot for teams
- best AI coding tools for startup teams
- how much are startups spending on AI tools
- AI subscription audit
- reduce AI tool spend

The product should therefore be framed around cost clarity and right-sizing, not generalized AI enthusiasm.

## Why the current product matches that wedge

The deployed screenshots make the positioning clearer:

- the landing page frames the product as a structured AI spend workspace, not a broad consulting site
- the audit form looks like a practical operating tool rather than a marketing gimmick
- the result page turns the output into a shareable decision surface

That is a good fit for the target user. A founder or eng manager is far more likely to trust a clean structured audit than a vague "AI optimization assistant."

## How I would get the first 100 users with no paid budget

The first 100 users should come from narrow, founder-heavy channels rather than broad awareness posting.

### 1. Founder and engineering-manager outbound

The simplest path is targeted outreach to people already talking publicly about:

- Cursor
- Copilot
- Claude
- ChatGPT team usage
- AI stack standardization

The message should not be "try my startup." It should be:

"I built a free AI spend audit for startup teams. If you want, I can run your stack through it and send you the result."

That is much lower friction and much more concrete.

### 2. Teardown-style content

The product lends itself well to short teardown posts such as:

- three ways startup teams overpay for AI tools
- what a 12-person team gets wrong about ChatGPT + Claude + Cursor spend
- when paying retail for AI tools stops making sense

This style of content is stronger than generic launch-post copy because it demonstrates the product's thinking model directly.

### 3. Community distribution

Good early channels:

- Indie Hackers
- founder and SaaS Slack groups
- startup and dev subreddits
- X / Twitter threads where founders complain about AI-tool sprawl

Again, the angle should be operational clarity, not shiny-product hype.

## Why this could be strategically useful for Credex

The best thing about this idea for Credex is that it can be more than content or lead capture.

Most spend calculators stop at:

- "you could save money"

BurnRate.ai can potentially stop at that in low-savings cases, but in higher-savings cases Credex has a stronger follow-up:

- help the team move to a cheaper or better-fit buying path
- help the team buy credits or usage more intelligently
- use the audit as a pre-qualification layer for a real commercial conversation

That makes the product:

- a utility
- a lead magnet
- a qualification filter

all at the same time.

## How I would define early traction

For week-one or week-two traction, I would not obsess over raw visits. The first proof would be:

- 100 completed audits
- 20 to 30 captured report submissions
- 5 to 10 high-savings cases over $500/month
- 3 or more consultation-worthy conversations

Those numbers are small in consumer terms, but they are meaningful for an early B2B wedge.

## What would make me believe the GTM is working

I would feel confident in this GTM if three things happen together:

1. people complete the audit without heavy hand-holding
2. a meaningful share/save rate appears on finished reports
3. the highest-savings cases line up with the types of teams Credex could actually help commercially

If those happen, then the product is not just a nice demo. It becomes a plausible acquisition surface.
